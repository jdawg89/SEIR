Overview:

The Susceptible-Exposed-Infected-Recovered (SEIR) model analyzes the dynamics of the spread of an epidemic. There are 4 groups or populations of people: the SUSCEPTIBLE are those that have never interacted with the pathogen (e.g. bacteria, virus, etc.) who have the potential to be infected. The EXPOSED are those that have encountered the pathogen, but are currently not contagious. In other words, these people do have the pathogen and may or may not have symptoms (depending on the disease process) but are unable to spread the pathogen to others. The INFECTED are those who have the pathogen, who may or may not have symptoms, and MAY spread the pathogen to others. Finally, the RECOVERED are those who cannot get the disease. These people may have had the disease in the past and are now currently immune, who have gotten a vaccine, or who are naturally resistant to the disease. In this model, infected individuals become recovered.

Instructions:
The boxes on the right side of the page control the parameters of the model. The page should load with some parameters already in the box. Click "submit" to run the model. The parameters can all be modified and the model re-run. The parameters are
\(\beta\)The parameter controlling how often a susceptible-infected contact results in a new exposure.
\(\gamma\)The rate an infected recovers and moves into the resistant phase.
\(\sigma\)The rate at which an exposed person becomes infective.
\(\mu\)The natural birth rate (this is unrelated to disease).
\(\delta\)The natural death rate (this is unrelated to disease).
\(\delta_d\)The death rate of infectious individuals.
\(\nu\)The rate a susceptible person gets vaccinated and becomes resistant (joins the Recovered group).
Initial susceptibleThe number of susceptible individuals at the beginning of the model run.
Initial exposedThe number of exposed individuals at the beginning of the model run.
Initial infectedThe number of infected individuals at the beginning of the model run.
Initial recoveredThe number of recovered individuals at the beginning of the model run.
DaysControls how long the model will run.
This program runs on your computer, so some computers may run faster than others. It is probably a good idea not to set the number iterations or the initial populations too high, since it will take longer to run. Note that cookies must be enabled for the algorithm to function.

Details:
This is an ordinary differential equation model, described by the following equation:
\[\begin{align*} \frac{dS}{dt} &= \mu N - \beta\frac{SI}{N} - (\delta + \nu) S \\ \frac{dE}{dt} &= \beta\frac{SI}{N} - (\delta + \sigma)E \\ \frac{dI}{dt} &= \sigma E - (\delta_d + \gamma)I \\ \frac{dR}{dt} &= \gamma I - \delta R + \nu S \\ N &= S + E + I + R \end{align*} \] The value \(R_0\) is known as the basic reproduction number and represents the expected number of new infections generated by a single case. In the SEIR model, this value is
\[R_0 = \frac{\sigma}{\delta+\sigma}\frac{\beta}{\delta_d + \gamma},\]
which degenerates to \(R_0 = \frac{\beta}{\gamma}\) when the death rates \(\delta = \delta_d = 0\). The simulation uses the fourth-order Runge-Kutta algorithm to solve it numerically, with a step size fixed at 0.01, written in JavaScript. The plotting methods are from the flot module. Both the ode simulation and the script in this page calling it are new, so there may still be some unanticipated bugs (I am also fairly new to the language, so my code may be inefficient or bizarre in places). Internet Explorer may not work since it has not yet adopted the canvas element, which is used in plotting.
